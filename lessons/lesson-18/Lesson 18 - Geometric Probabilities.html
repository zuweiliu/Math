Geometric Probabilities

When outcomes are infinite, we stop counting and start measuring — with length, area, and volume. Drag the sliders, run the simulations, and watch the thinking path light up.

The Big Idea

Geometric probability is used when there are infinitely many equally‑likely outcomes. We can't list them, so we measure them instead.

\[ P(\text{event})=\frac{\text{measure of the successful region}}{\text{measure of the whole sample region}} \]

The #1 skill: turn the words into a picture of the sample space, shade the "success" part, then take the ratio.

How many random quantities? → which measure?

Random quantitiesSample space looks likeMeasure
1 (one number / one cut / one arrival time)an interval (a line segment)length
2 (two numbers / two cuts)a square / region in the planearea

a random point in a shapethe shape itselfarea (or arc length on a curve)

Section 1 — Probability Using Lengths

\[ P=\frac{\text{length of successful region}}{\text{length of possible region}} \]

Example 1.1 — Cut a segment so both parts are long enough

A segment of length 1 is cut at a random point. Find \(P(\text{both parts}\ge \tfrac13)\). Drag the cut below.

Green band = the success window where both pieces are \(\ge\tfrac13\). The cut wins only when it lands inside it.
Let the cut be \(x\sim\text{Uniform}[0,1]\). Sample space length \(=1\).
Pieces are \(x\) and \(1-x\). Need both \(\ge\tfrac13\): \(x\ge\tfrac13\) and \(1-x\ge\tfrac13\Rightarrow \tfrac13\le x\le\tfrac23\).
Success length \(=\tfrac23-\tfrac13=\tfrac13\). So \(P=\dfrac{1/3}{1}=\) 1/3.
🔑 TYPE — One random cut on a segment (1 variable → length)
METHOD: Let the cut \(=x\) on \([0,1]\). Write each condition as an inequality in \(x\), intersect them into one sub‑interval, then \(P=\dfrac{\text{sub-interval length}}{\text{total length}}\).

Example 1.2 — Where is an inequality satisfied?

\(x\) is chosen at random on \([-5,5]\). Find \(P(x^2-x-2>0)\).

The number line \([-5,5]\). Green = where \((x-2)(x+1)>0\). The parabola above shows when it's positive (above the axis).
Total length \(=10\).
Factor: \(x^2-x-2=(x-2)(x+1)>0\Rightarrow x<-1\) or \(x>2\).
Intersect with \([-5,5]\): \([-5,-1)\) length \(4\), and \((2,5]\) length \(3\). Total \(=7\).
\(P=\dfrac{7}{10}=\) 0.7.
🔑 TYPE — One random number, condition = an inequality
METHOD: Total length = interval length. Solve the inequality to find which sub‑intervals work, intersect with the given interval, add their lengths, divide.

★ Example 1.3 — "Whose number is bigger?" 2017 AMC 10A #15

Chloé picks a real number uniformly from \([0,2017]\); Laurent picks uniformly from \([0,4034]\). Find \(P(\text{Laurent}>\text{Chloé})\).

The thinking path. Two random numbers ⇒ 2‑D area model. Step through it — the diagram and the checklist light up together.

Step 1. Draw the sample space: a rectangle. Chloé \(C\) is the width \([0,2017]\); Laurent \(L\) is the height \([0,4034]\). It is twice as tall as wide.
Step 2. The event \(L>C\) is split by the line \(L=C\). This line only reaches up to \(L=2017\) — it dies halfway up.
Step 3. The entire top band \(2017\le L\le 4034\) always has \(L>C\). Area \(=2017^2\). (Free win!)
Step 4. The bottom square is cut by \(L=C\) into two equal triangles. The upper one is \(L>C\): area \(=\tfrac12\,2017^2\).
Step 5. Success \(=2017^2+\tfrac12 2017^2=\tfrac32 2017^2\). Total \(=2017\cdot4034=2\cdot2017^2\). Ratio \(=\dfrac{3/2}{2}=\dfrac34\).
Empirical \(P(L>C)\): run the trials to watch it converge to 0.75.
Answer: \(P=\dfrac{\tfrac32\,2017^2}{2\cdot2017^2}=\) 3/4  (C). Intuition: Laurent's range is bigger, so the probability beats \(\tfrac12\).
🔑 TYPE — Two independent uniform numbers, compare them (area model)
METHOD: Make a rectangle with the two ranges as the two axes. The condition (e.g. \(L>C\)) is a line; shade the correct side. \(P=\dfrac{\text{shaded area}}{\text{rectangle area}}\). The trick here is noticing the rectangle is \(1:2\), so the line "runs out" halfway and a whole band is a guaranteed win.

Example 1.4 — Random point on a circle: acute triangle

\(A,B\) lie on a circle of radius 2 with chord \(AB=2\). A point \(P\) is chosen at random on the circle. Find \(P(\triangle APB\text{ is acute})\). Drag \(P\) or press Animate.

First — what is \(\theta\), and where is it? \(\theta\) is the central angle \(\angle AOB\) that the chord \(AB\) "opens up" at the center \(O\). The chord‑length formula gives it: \[ AB=2R\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\;\Rightarrow\;2=2(2)\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\;\Rightarrow\;\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}=\tfrac12\;\Rightarrow\;\theta=60^\circ. \] So the arc \(AB\) is \(60^\circ\). That 60° wedge is drawn in gold at \(O\) below.
Gold wedge at \(O\) = central angle θ = 60°. The two blue points are the boundaries: at P₁ the angle \(\angle P_1AB=90^\circ\) (so \(AP_1\perp AB\)), and at P₂ the angle \(\angle P_2BA=90^\circ\) (so \(BP_2\perp AB\)). The triangle is acute only when \(P\) is on the green arc between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) — drag past either one and that corner's angle jumps over \(90^\circ\). That valid arc is \(60^\circ\) → \(P=\tfrac16\).
Find \(\theta\) (the gold wedge). Chord \(AB=2R\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\): \(2=2(2)\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\Rightarrow \sin\frac{\theta}{2}=\tfrac12\Rightarrow\theta=60^\circ\). So minor arc \(AB=60^\circ\) and the inscribed angle at \(P\) (whenever \(P\) is on the big arc) is always \(\theta/2=30^\circ\).
Acute rule. An inscribed angle \(=\tfrac12\) its opposite arc, so an angle \(<90^\circ\) ⇔ its opposite arc \(<180^\circ\). Acute ⇔ all three arcs \(<180^\circ\) ⇔ center \(O\) is inside the triangle.
Find the two boundary points. The angle at \(A\) equals \(90^\circ\) when \(AP\perp AB\) — call that point \(P_1\). The angle at \(B\) equals \(90^\circ\) when \(BP\perp AB\) — call that \(P_2\). Both are drawn in blue. \(P\) gives an acute triangle only between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\); outside, the angle at \(A\) or \(B\) exceeds \(90^\circ\). That arc is \(300-120-120=60^\circ\).
\(P=\dfrac{60^\circ}{360^\circ}=\) 1/6.
🔑 TYPE — Random point on a circle; acute/obtuse triangle
METHOD: \(P=\dfrac{\text{favorable arc}}{360^\circ}\). Use the theorem: an inscribed triangle is acute ⇔ all three arcs \(<180^\circ\) (⇔ center inside). Convert each "acute" requirement into an arc inequality and add the allowed arc.

Section 2 — Probability Using Areas

\[ P=\frac{\text{area of successful region}}{\text{area of total region}} \]

Example 2.1 — Cut a stick into three short pieces

A 1‑m stick is cut at two random points into three pieces. Find \(P(\text{every piece}<\tfrac12)\). Drag the two cuts.

Why area and not length? Count the random quantities. Here there are two independent cuts, \(x\) and \(y\). Two free numbers ⇒ we need two axes to draw every outcome ⇒ the sample space is a 2‑D square, so we measure it with area. (One random number would be a 1‑D segment → length; three would be a 3‑D cube → volume.)
The stick with your two cuts. A piece turns red when it reaches \(\tfrac12\) — that's a losing cut.
Same situation as a map: every pair of cuts \((x,y)\) is one dot in this unit square. Green region = "all three pieces \(<\tfrac12\)". The white dot is your current cut. Empirical \(P\) → 1/4 = 0.25.
Two random numbers → area. Cut positions \(x,y\) are each uniform on \([0,1]\) and independent. Plot \(x\) across, \(y\) up: every outcome is a point in the unit square, total area \(=1\) (this is the denominator).
Name the pieces. If the smaller cut is \(\ell=\min(x,y)\) and larger is \(h=\max(x,y)\), the three pieces are \(\ell,\; h-\ell,\; 1-h\).
Turn "each \(<\tfrac12\)" into lines. \(\ell<\tfrac12\), \(h-\ell<\tfrac12\), \(1-h<\tfrac12\) (i.e. \(h>\tfrac12\)). These are straight‑line boundaries, so the success set is a region — we take its area.
Shade and measure. The region is two triangles (one for \(xy\)), each with legs \(\tfrac12\): area \(=2\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac12=\tfrac14\).
\(P=\dfrac{\text{green area}}{\text{square area}}=\dfrac{1/4}{1}=\) 1/4. (Bonus: "all pieces \(<\tfrac12\)" is exactly the condition the three pieces can form a triangle.)
🔑 TYPE — Two cuts on a stick (2 variables → area)
METHOD: Count the random numbers first — two ⇒ unit square ⇒ area. Put the cuts as \((x,y)\), write each "piece \(<\tfrac12\)" as a line, shade the region, and divide its area by the square's area. Memorize the result: 3 pieces of a unit stick are all \(<\tfrac12\) (⇔ form a triangle) with probability \(\tfrac14\).

Example 2.2 — Three random points on a circle form an acute triangle

Pick 3 points at random on a circle. Find \(P(\text{triangle is acute})\). Drag the three points.

Why area — and why this is secretly Example 2.1. Fix one point (the circle looks the same after a rotation, so this is free). The other two points are two independent random angles ⇒ two free numbers ⇒ a 2‑D square ⇒ area. And here's the punch line: cut the circle open at the fixed point and unroll it into a stick of length 1 — the other two points become two cuts, and the three arcs become the three pieces. So this is exactly the stick problem!
Why "every arc \(<\tfrac12\)" means acute — the inscribed‑angle theorem (same tool as 1.4). The three points cut the circle into three arcs. Each corner of the triangle is an inscribed angle, and the inscribed‑angle theorem says it equals half the arc it opens onto (the arc on the far side, between the other two points): \[ \angle(\text{at point }1)=\tfrac12\,\overset{\frown}{23},\qquad \angle 2=\tfrac12\,\overset{\frown}{31},\qquad \angle 3=\tfrac12\,\overset{\frown}{12}. \] A corner is \(<90^\circ\) exactly when its arc is \(<180^\circ\) — i.e. less than a semicircle (\(<50\%\) of the circle). So: \[ \text{acute (all 3 corners }<90^\circ)\iff \text{all 3 arcs }<\text{ a semicircle}. \] In the diagram each corner now shows its live angle (= half its opposite arc); push any arc to \(50\%\) and that opposite corner hits \(90^\circ\) and turns red.
Second way — the Example 1.4 trick: "is the center \(O\) inside the triangle?" There's a slicker geometric test that needs no angle arithmetic. A triangle inscribed in a circle is acute ⇔ the center \(O\) lies inside it. Reason: an arc reaches a full semicircle exactly when its chord passes through \(O\); if some arc is \(\ge\) a semicircle, the whole triangle sits on one side of that diameter, pushing \(O\) outside (and that corner becomes \(\ge 90^\circ\)). So "all arcs \(<\tfrac12\)" and "\(O\) inside" are the same statement. Watch the dot at \(O\): it's green inside for acute, red the instant \(O\) slips outside — just like 1.4, where the triangle was acute only while the center stayed enclosed.
Three draggable points cut the circle into three arcs. Each corner shows its angle (\(=\tfrac12\) the opposite arc). The triangle is acute exactly when every arc is \(<50\%\) — equivalently when the center \(O\) stays inside. Push an arc to \(50\%\) and its opposite corner hits \(90^\circ\) (turns red) while \(O\) crosses the edge.
Yes — the \(P_1,P_2\) boundary trick from 1.4 works here too. In 1.4 we fixed the chord \(AB\) and let one point \(P\) roam; \(P\) gave an acute triangle only on the arc between two boundary points \(P_1\) (where \(\angle A=90^\circ\)) and \(P_2\) (where \(\angle B=90^\circ\)). Do exactly the same here: fix points 1 and 2, let point \(P\) be the third. Then \[ \angle 1=90^\circ \Leftrightarrow 2P\text{ is a diameter} \Leftrightarrow P=\text{antipode of }2\;(=P_1),\qquad \angle 2=90^\circ \Leftrightarrow P=\text{antipode of }1\;(=P_2). \] So \(P_1,P_2\) are just the diametrically‑opposite points of the two fixed vertices. The triangle is acute only when \(P\) lies on the green arc between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) (the side away from chord 1–2). The third angle \(\angle P\) is automatically \(<90^\circ\) there, because arc 1–2 is the small \(120^\circ\) arc — exactly the 1.4 situation, just with a different fixed chord. Drag \(P\) past \(P_1\) or \(P_2\) and that vertex's angle jumps over \(90^\circ\).
1, 2 are fixed (the chord). P₁ = antipode of 2 (here \(\angle 1=90^\circ\)); P₂ = antipode of 1 (here \(\angle 2=90^\circ\)) — the dashed lines are the two diameters \(\,2P_1\) and \(1P_2\). Drag P: acute (green) only on the arc between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\), just like 1.4.
The unrolled view: arc to point 2 = \(x\), arc to point 3 = \(y\). Identical green region as Example 2.1. White dot = your current triangle. Empirical \(P\) → 1/4 = 0.25.
Why each arc \(<\tfrac12\) ⇔ acute. Each corner is an inscribed angle \(=\tfrac12\) its opposite arc, so corner \(<90^\circ\) ⇔ opposite arc \(<180^\circ\) (a semicircle). All three corners acute ⇔ all three arcs \(<\) a semicircle. (Second way, same as 1.4: equivalently the center \(O\) lies inside the triangle.)
Fix one point → two random numbers. By rotation, anchor point 1. Let \(x,y\) be the arc‑fractions to points 2 and 3 (each uniform on \([0,1]\)). Two numbers ⇒ unit square, area 1.
Unroll = stick. The three arcs are \(\min(x,y),\; |x-y|,\; 1-\max(x,y)\) — the same three pieces as Example 2.1. "All arcs \(<\tfrac12\)" is the same green region.
Measure. Two triangles, area \(\tfrac14\). \(P(\text{acute})=\) 1/4, so \(P(\text{obtuse})=\tfrac34\) (right angle has area 0).
🔑 TYPE — Several random points on a circle; triangle shape
METHOD: Convert the angle condition to an arc condition with the inscribed‑angle theorem (acute ⇔ every arc \(<180^\circ\) ⇔ center \(O\) inside). Fix one point by symmetry so only two random numbers remain ⇒ area in the unit square. Recognize it as the stick problem ⇒ \(\tfrac14\).

Example 3.1 — Linear condition on two random numbers

\(x,y\) chosen at random from \([-2,2]\). Find \(P(x+y>1)\).

Green dots satisfy \(x+y>1\). Empirical \(P\) → converges to 9/32 ≈ 0.281.
Sample space = the \(4\times4\) square (area 16). Success = triangle above the line \(x+y=1\).
Square area \(=4\times4=16\).
Line \(x+y=1\) cuts off the triangle with vertices \((-1,2),(2,2),(2,-1)\): legs \(3,3\), area \(=\tfrac12(3)(3)=\tfrac92\).
\(P=\dfrac{9/2}{16}=\) 9/32.
🔑 TYPE — Two uniform numbers + a linear condition (area)
METHOD: Draw the square, draw the boundary line, shade the correct side, use \(\tfrac12 bh\) for triangles. \(P=\dfrac{\text{shaded}}{\text{square}}\).

Example 3.2 — Obtuse triangle from random sides 2018 AMC 10B #22

\(x,y\) chosen independently and uniformly from \([0,1]\). Which value is closest to \(P(x,y,1\text{ form an obtuse triangle})\)?

Green = obtuse triangle. Empirical \(P\) → converges to about 0.29.
Unit square. Below the line \(x+y=1\): no triangle. Inside the quarter‑circle \(x^2+y^2<1\) (and a valid triangle): the angle opposite side \(1\) is obtuse.
Need a real triangle: \(x+y>1\).
Side \(1\) is longest; obtuse ⇔ \(x^2+y^2<1\). Region = inside quarter circle, above the line.
Area \(=\dfrac{\pi}{4}-\dfrac12\approx0.285\Rightarrow P\approx\) 0.29 (C).

Example 4.1 — Within \(d\) of a lattice point 2020 AMC 10A #16

A point is chosen at random in the \(2020\times2020\) square. \(P(\text{within }d\text{ of a lattice point})=\tfrac12\). Find \(d\) (nearest tenth). Drag the slider.

The grid repeats every \(1\times1\) cell. Four corner quarter‑circles assemble into one full circle of area \(\pi d^2\). Set \(\pi d^2=\tfrac12\).
Reduce to one unit cell. Covered area \(=4\times\tfrac14\pi d^2=\pi d^2\).
\(\pi d^2=\tfrac12\Rightarrow d^2=\dfrac1{2\pi}\approx0.159\Rightarrow d\approx0.399\).
\(d\approx\) 0.4 (B).
🔑 TYPE — Random point in a grid; "within \(d\) of a lattice point"
METHOD: Reduce to ONE unit cell by periodicity. Corner quarter‑circles assemble into a full circle \(\pi d^2\). Set \(\pi d^2=(\text{given probability})\) and solve.

Example 4.2 — Chord intersects the inner circle 2014 AMC 10B #19

Concentric circles, radii 1 and 2. Two points on the outer circle are chosen at random. Find \(P(\text{the chord intersects the inner circle})\). Drag the second point.

Fix the first point (symmetry). The chord hits the inner circle ⇔ distance from center \(=2\cos\frac{\varphi}{2}<1\) ⇔ \(\varphi\in(120^\circ,240^\circ)\). Chord turns green when it crosses.
Fix point 1; gap angle \(\varphi\sim\text{Uniform}[0,360^\circ)\).
Distance center→chord \(=2\cos\frac{\varphi}{2}<1\Rightarrow\cos\frac{\varphi}{2}<\tfrac12\Rightarrow\varphi\in(120^\circ,240^\circ)\).
\(P=\dfrac{240-120}{360}=\) 1/3 (B).
🔑 TYPE — Two random points on a circle; chord vs. inner region
METHOD: Fix one point (rotational symmetry); the gap angle \(\varphi\) is the single uniform variable. Turn the geometry into an inequality via distance center→chord \(=R\cos\frac{\varphi}{2}\). Then \(P=\dfrac{\text{good angle}}{360^\circ}\).

Universal 4‑step recipe
  1. Identify the random quantity(ies) → pick length / area / volume.
  2. Draw the sample space and find its measure.
  3. Translate every condition into inequalities / regions; shade success.
  4. \(P=\dfrac{\text{success measure}}{\text{sample measure}}\). Use the complement when "success" is messy.
