Preview - Basic Ways of Factorization

Factoring means rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials. Before the advanced tricks, make sure these are automatic:

Warm-up Check

Factor \(50x^2-72\). Which is the first move?

TYPE - Basics review
METHOD: \(50x^2-72=2(25x^2-36)=2(5x-6)(5x+6)\). Always GCF first; squares appear after.

Concept 1 - Factoring by Grouping

When a polynomial has four or more terms and no overall GCF, group terms so each group shares a factor. Two common shapes: 2-2 grouping and 3-1 grouping (often a hidden perfect square plus a leftover).

Preview Example 1 - 2-2 grouping

Factor \(x^3+3x^2+2x+6\).

Group: \((x^3+3x^2)+(2x+6)\).
Factor each group: \(x^2(x+3)+2(x+3)\).
Common binomial \((x+3)\): \((x+3)(x^2+2)\).
TYPE - 2-2 grouping
METHOD: Split four terms into two pairs that each factor, then pull out the shared binomial.

Preview Example 2 - 3-1 grouping (square + difference of squares)

Factor \(x^2+6x+9-y^2\).

Group the 3 perfect-square terms: \((x^2+6x+9)-y^2\).
That's \((x+3)^2-y^2\), a difference of squares.
Answer: \((x+3-y)(x+3+y)\).
TYPE - 3-1 grouping
METHOD: Spot three terms that form a perfect square, then use \(A^2-B^2\) with the leftover term.

Concept 2 - Splitting & Adding Terms (Completing Squares)

Splitting: rewrite one term as a sum (e.g. \(3a^2=a^2+2a^2\)). Adding: add and subtract the same quantity so a perfect square appears. Then finish with a difference of squares \(A^2-B^2=(A-B)(A+B)\).

Preview Example 3 - add & subtract to build a square

Factor \(x^4+4\) (the classic Sophie Germain shape).

Add and subtract \(4x^2\): \(x^4+4x^2+4-4x^2\).
First three terms form a square: \((x^2+2)^2-(2x)^2\).
Difference of squares: \((x^2-2x+2)(x^2+2x+2)\).
TYPE - Add/subtract a middle term
METHOD: To make \(A^2-B^2\), add the missing \(2AB\) term and subtract it back as a perfect square.

Preview Example 4 - split the middle term

Factor \(a^2-6a+8\) by splitting \(-6a\).

Need product \(8\), sum \(-6\): use \(-2\) and \(-4\).
Split: \(a^2-2a-4a+8 = a(a-2)-4(a-2)\).
Answer: \((a-2)(a-4)\).
TYPE - Split the middle term
METHOD: Replace \(bx\) by two terms whose product is \(ac\) and sum is \(b\), then group.

Concept 3 - Simon's Favorite Factoring Trick

For expressions like \(xy+ax+by\), add the constant \(ab\) so it factors:

\[ xy+ax+by+ab=(x+b)(y+a) \]

Great for equations in two positive-integer unknowns: factor, then test divisor pairs.

Preview Example 5 - integer solutions via SFFT

Positive integers \(x,y\) satisfy \(xy+2x+y=20\). Find all pairs \((x,y)\).

Rewrite with the \(y\)-group: \(xy+2x+y = x(y+2)+(y+2)-2\).
So \((x+1)(y+2)=22\).
Positive divisor pairs of \(22\) with \(x+1\ge 2,\ y+2\ge 3\): \((x+1,y+2)=(2,11)\) or \((11,2)\).
\((2,11)\Rightarrow(x,y)=(1,9)\); \((11,2)\) gives \(y=0\) (rejected). Answer: \((x,y)=(1,9)\).
TYPE - SFFT integer equation
METHOD: Add the constant that completes \((x+b)(y+a)\), then match positive-divisor pairs to the right-hand number.

Concept 4 - Extreme Values by Completing Squares

Since a square is never negative (\(A^2\ge 0\)), writing an expression as (squares) + constant reveals its minimum instantly.

Preview Example 6 - minimum of a quadratic

Find the least value of \(x^2+6x+11\).

Complete the square: \(x^2+6x+9+2=(x+3)^2+2\).
\((x+3)^2\ge 0\), smallest when \(x=-3\).
Least value: 2.
TYPE - Min via completing the square
METHOD: Force a perfect square; the leftover constant is the minimum and the square's zero gives the location.

Preview Example 7 - two-variable minimum

Find the least value of \(x^2+2y^2-2xy+4y+7\).

Group \(x\)-terms: \((x^2-2xy+y^2)+y^2+4y+7=(x-y)^2+(y^2+4y+7)\).
Complete again: \(y^2+4y+7=(y+2)^2+3\).
So expression \(=(x-y)^2+(y+2)^2+3\ge 3\), at \(y=-2,\ x=-2\). Least: 3.
TYPE - Sum of squares minimum
METHOD: Complete the square in one variable at a time until only \(\ge0\) squares plus a constant remain.
